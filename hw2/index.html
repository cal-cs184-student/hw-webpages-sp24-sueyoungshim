Part 1 Bezier curves with 1D de Casteljau subdivision

De Casteljau’s algorithm is a recursive method for evaluating Bezier curves. The algorithm works by linearly interpolating between points, reducing the number of points at each step until only one point remains. This point is on the Bezier curve for the given parameter t value. The process involves computing intermediate points based on the parameter t (where 0 <= t <= 1), which represents the position along the curve.

​​In my implementation, I developed a function evaluateStep to process a step in de Casteljau's algorithm. This function iterates through a set of input points, linearly interpolating between consecutive points based on a parameter t. The interpolation formula (1 - t) * point_i + t * point_i+1 is used to calculate intermediate points, which are stored in the  vector. This vector is returned and can be used iteratively to approximate the point on the Bezier curve.

Part 2 Bezier surfaces with separable 1D de Casteljiau

De Casteljau's algorithm extends naturally to Bezier surfaces by applying the algorithm in two dimensions. For Bezier surfaces, the algorithm iteratively interpolates points not only along one parameter but across a grid of control points using two parameters, u and v. This process involves first interpolating points along one direction for a fixed value of the other parameter and then interpolating the results along the second direction. This dual application effectively constructs the surface point by point.

In my implementation for evaluating Bezier surfaces, I started with the evaluateStep function, which linearly interpolates between consecutive 3D points based on a parameter t, extending the concept directly from curves to surface evaluation. This function forms the basis for the evaluate1D method, which recursively applies evaluateStep to a vector of 3D points until it reduces them to a single point, representing an interpolation along one direction.

For evaluating the entire Bezier patch, the evaluate function takes two parameters, u and v, representing the position on the Bezier surface. It first iterates over the sets of control points along one direction using parameter u to generate intermediate points for each row. These intermediate points are then treated as control points for a second application of evaluate1D, this time using the parameter v, to interpolate between the results of the first interpolation. This two-step process effectively evaluates the surface at the point (u, v), yielding the final 3D point on the Bezier surface.

Part 3 Area-weighted vertex normals

In my method for computing area-weighted vertex normals, I calculated an approximate unit normal for each vertex by averaging the normals of adjacent triangles, weighted by their areas, and then normalizing this average. This process involves iterating over the half-edges linked to the vertex to visit each neighboring triangle. I started with a halfedge from the vertex, looping through the halfedges to cover each adjacent triangle. For every triangle, I find the cross product of vectors formed by its vertices (v1, v2, and v3), which gives a vector perpendicular to the triangle's surface. This vector's length is proportional to the triangle's area, making it ideal for the area-weighted average. After accumulating these vectors for all neighboring triangles, I normalize the sum to obtain the final unit normal vector. This method effectively smoothens the vertex normal by considering the geometric complexity of the surrounding mesh.

Part 5 Edge split

For the edge split operation, I introduced a series of new geometric elements: 3 edges, 1 vertex, 2 faces, and 6 halfedges. This expansion was essential to accurately divide an existing edge into two, while also ensuring that the mesh topology remains consistent and coherent.

To implement this, I first calculated the midpoint of the target edge to position the new vertex. Then, I updated the pointers between existing and new elements. This included reassigning halfedge pointers to maintain the integrity of faces and vertices. A significant part of this process involved ensuring that the new vertex's halfedge correctly pointed along the original edge, which was split, to preserve the mesh's navigability.

While implementing the edgeSplit operation, I faced a similar issue that I had with the edgeFlip, where the adjacent faces disappeared upon splitting the edge. To debug this, I used a similar method where I drew out a diagram of the mesh and counted all the elements to ensure the correct number of vertices, edges, faces, and halfedges were present post-operation. Additionally, I paid close attention to the reassignment of the halfedge pointers for all involved mesh components.

Part 6 Loop subdivision for mesh upsampling

To implement the loop subdivision in our mesh, I followed a structured approach detailed in five main steps, ensuring a smooth transition from the coarse mesh to a refined one. The first step involved recalculating the positions for all vertices based on Loop's subdivision rules. Each vertex's new position was determined by considering its original location and the average positions of its neighboring vertices. A unique weighting scheme was applied, where the weight u varied depending on the vertex degree. Next, for every edge, a new vertex position was computed at the edge's midpoint, adjusted by the positions of the adjacent vertices to smooth the geometry. The calculated positions were temporarily stored, ready to be applied to newly inserted vertices at the edges' midpoints. The third step was splitting the edges and creating new vertices at their calculated positions. A key challenge here was ensuring that only original edges were split, preventing an infinite loop of splitting newly created edges. To manage this, edges were marked as new or original, and a list of new edges was maintained to keep track of the evolving mesh structure. In the fourth step, edge flips were applied to edges that connected an old and a new vertex, further enhancing the mesh's smoothness and symmetry. This step was critical for redistributing the mesh's topology, ensuring that vertices were evenly spaced, and the mesh surface smoothly transitioned across the subdivided areas. The final step was updating each vertex's position to its newly calculated location. This final step solidified the transformation, embedding the new, more refined geometry into the mesh, ready for rendering or further processing.

One of the bugs that I encountered was in the edge split and flip process. I was not checking if the edge that I’m flipping is the original edge or newly created edge. For example, in the diagram above, I marked  the orange edge to be true for isNew during edge split to avoid infinite loop. Those orange edges connect the old vertex and new vertex, but it’s not supposed to get flipped. To check for this additional condition was by saving the new blue edges in a list and later only flipped if the edge is inside the list, is new, and is connecting the old and the new.

One of the most notable observations of loop subdivision is the smoothing of sharp corners and edges. As the subdivision process iteratively interpolates and refines the mesh geometry, sharp angles are gradually softened, leading to a more rounded appearance. This is inherent to the subdivision algorithm, which averages vertex positions to create a smoother surface.

I can reduce this effect by pre-splitting some edges. Pre-splitting mitigates the effect because loop subdivision works by averaging the positions of existing vertices to create new ones, which inherently smoothens the mesh. By pre-splitting, additional vertices and edges are created into specific areas of the mesh. When subdivision process averages the positions of these vertices, the presence of more closely spaced vertices around sharp features allows for a finer control over the averaging process. This can help in maintaining the distinctiveness of sharp edges or corners by creating a denser mesh topology.

Screenshots of symmetrical cube

When performing several iterations of loop subdivision on a cube, the cube may become slightly asymmetric after repeated subdivisions. This asymmetry occurs due to the inherent nature of the loop subdivision process, which averages vertex positions to create a smoother surface. The original mesh has vertices, edges, or faces that are not evenly distributed, the averaging process can introduce slight deviations in symmetry over multiple iterations. This effect is accentuated in a cube because the vertices at the corners are averaged with a different neighborhood configuration than those along the edges or faces, leading to an uneven distribution of vertices over iterations.

To address this issue and achieve a symmetric subdivision, I pre-processed the cube by splitting the diagonal edges of all its faces, effectively converting each square face into three triangular faces. This pre-processing step is helped alleviate the effects because the newly created vertices help in achieving a more even distribution across the faces of the cube. When the loop subdivision is applied afterward, the averaging of vertex positions occurs in a more uniform manner, as the vertices are now more evenly spaced. This uniform behavior helps in maintaining the cube's symmetry as it is subdivided, counteracting the asymmetry that might arise from the original square faces.
