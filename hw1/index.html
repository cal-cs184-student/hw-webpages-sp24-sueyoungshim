Task 1: Drawing Single-Color Triangles

For rasterizing triangles, my approach was to first identify the bounding box surrounding the triangle. This was crucial because it allowed me to limit my focus to a smaller area of the screen, significantly reducing unnecessary computations. To determine whether a pixel falls inside the triangle, I incremented both x and y coordinates by 0.5 to ensure I was checking the pixel center. To handle triangles regardless of their winding order, I adapted the point-in-triangle test. I checked if the signs of areas formed by the triangle's vertices and the pixel were consistent (all positive or all negative), which indicated that the pixel lies within the triangle. Once the pixel passed the test, I filled it with the input color.

My algorithm's efficiency comes from directly targeting the triangle's bounding box, avoiding checks on pixels that couldn't possibly be inside the triangle. This direct approach is as efficient as any method that examines each sample within the bounding box, as it minimizes the number of calculations needed to determine which pixels need coloring.

Task 2: Antialiasing by Supersampling

Supersampling is a technique for smoothing out the edges in my rendered images, essentially acting as an antialiasing method. By dividing each pixel into multiple sub-pixels (determined by the sampling rate), I was able to calculate a more nuanced color value that took into account the colors of these sub-pixels. This approach was particularly effective in reducing the jagged edges resulting in a much smoother appearance.

I integrated supersampling into my rasterization pipeline by adjusting the algorithm to account for these smaller sub-pixels within each pixel's area. Instead of immediately coloring a pixel after determining it falls within a triangle, I first sampled the sub-pixels inside the sample buffer and then averaged the values, and populated them into the frame buffer, which is what eventually gets rendered.

To demonstrate the effectiveness of supersampling, I rendered <code>basic/test4.svg</code> with varying sample rates. The comparisons clearly showed that as the sample rate increased, the edges of shapes became significantly smoother, showcasing the power of supersampling in enhancing image quality.

The accompanying photos demonstrate how varying sample rates impact the rendering quality of a triangle's slender edge. When the sample rate is set to 1, the edge of the triangle is noticeably jagged and exhibits clear disconnections, with the pixels displaying a uniform coloration. However, as we increase the sample rate, some pixels begin to have a lighter hue. This subtle shift in color is the result of averaging the color values of the sub-pixels within each pixel area. Essentially, this process blends white with the original color red, producing a spectrum of softer pink shades. Such a blending effect softens the previously broken edges, resulting in a smoother edge appearance. This enhanced rendering technique underscores the critical role of supersampling in refining the aesthetic quality of digital images, offering a striking contrast between low and high fidelity edge rendering.

Task 5: “Pixel Sampling” for texture mapping

Pixel sampling is a process of selecting specific texels from a texture map to be mapped on to the corresponding pixels. One of the methods for selecting a texel is the nearest pixel sampling. When rendering a texture or scaling an image, this method selects the color of the nearest texel to the target pixel's position. It does not perform any averaging or blending of neighboring pixels. As a result, this method is very fast and requires minimal computational resources.

However, the downside of nearest pixel sampling is its tendency to produce images with a blocky or pixelated appearance, especially when the texture or image is magnified significantly. This is because it does not smooth out the transitions between texels.

Another method is the bilinear pixel sampling. It’s a more sophisticated method that improves upon the visual quality offered by nearest pixel sampling. It takes into account the colors of the four nearest texels surrounding the target pixel and calculates the output color by performing a linear interpolation between these texels, both horizontally and vertically. This process results in smoother transitions and less blocky images compared to nearest pixel sampling.

Bilinear sampling effectively blends the colors of adjacent texels, which can significantly reduce the appearance of jagged edges and pixelation, especially in magnified textures or images. The trade-off is that bilinear sampling requires more computational power than nearest pixel sampling, due to the additional calculations involved in the interpolation process.

Increasing the sample rate while using nearest neighbor sampling smoothed out some of the pixelation since multiple samples are taken within each pixel area, and their colors are averaged. However, since it's still based on the nearest sampling method, the result can still be somewhat blocky compared to bilinear sampling, but less so than with only 1 sample per pixel.

This method provides the smoothest image of all the techniques. Bilinear interpolation, combined with a higher sample rate, will create very smooth color transitions. The higher sample rate allows for more color values to be averaged within each pixel area, reducing aliasing and improving the overall image quality.

Task 6: “Level Sampling” with mipmaps for texture mapping

Level sampling is about choosing the appropriate mipmap level for a given pixel on a rendered object. The "level" in level sampling refers to the specific version of a texture that is most appropriate for the pixel's screen size. In mipmapping, multiple pre-computed versions of a texture are stored, each at a different resolution. The highest resolution is level zero, used when the texture is viewed up close. As the object gets farther away, lower resolution versions are used, which are pre-calculated to be less detailed and thus use fewer computational resources.

I’ve implemented this method by first going through each pixel inside the triangle just like the previous tasks. Then I mapped the current pixel (x, y) to its corresponding uv-coordinates along with (x+1, y) and (x, y+1). I was able to correspond the pixels to texels by calculating the barycentric coordinates. With the uv coordinates, I computed the appropriate mipmap level by calculating the maximum spatial derivative in texture space and converting into a mipmap level using log. Then it was just applying the level to the pixel sampling function. There were 3 different kinds of level sampling: level 0, nearest level, linear level. Nearest level was taking the nearest integer level of the computed level. Linear level was taking a weighted sum of the lower level and upper level, leading to a much smoother texture.

Tradeoffs between speed, memory usage, and antialiasing power between pixel sampling, level sampling, or the number of samples per pixel

Pixel Sampling is a choice between the speed of nearest pixel sampling and the quality of bilinear pixel sampling. Nearest pixel sampling is the quicker of the two, requiring less computational power as it simply takes the color of the nearest texel. Bilinear pixel sampling, although slower, provides superior antialiasing by averaging the colors of the four closest texels for a smoother result. While pixel sampling affects rendering speed and quality, it does not have a direct impact on memory usage, as it does not necessitate additional texture resolutions.

Level Sampling optimizes the rendering process. By using different levels of texture detail depending on the object's size on the screen, level sampling can increase rendering speeds. This is because lower-resolution textures are used when objects are small or far away. However, the downside is an increased memory requirement by 33%, as multiple versions of each texture must be stored. In terms of antialiasing, level sampling can significantly reduce visual artifacts like moiré patterns when textures are viewed at steep angles or from a distance, thus contributing to a smoother visual experience.

Supersampling has the most significant impact on both speed and memory. Increasing the number of samples means more color values must be computed and stored, which can slow down the rendering process and demand more memory. However, the benefit of supersampling is its superior antialiasing power. By averaging the colors of multiple samples within each pixel, it effectively reduces the jagged edges and provides the highest quality visual output among the three techniques.
